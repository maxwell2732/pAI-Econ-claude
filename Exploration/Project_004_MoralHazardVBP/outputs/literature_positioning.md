## Theoretical Tradition

This research draws on the following branches of economic theory:

**Primary tradition:** Health Insurance Economics / Moral Hazard in Insurance Markets
**Foundational framework:** The demand-side moral hazard framework established by Pauly (1968) and Zeckhauser (1970), which models insurance coverage as a reduction in the effective marginal price of care, leading to over-consumption relative to the social optimum. Arrow (1963) provided the broader welfare framework for medical care markets under uncertainty.

**Secondary traditions:**
1. Procurement and Regulation Theory — specifically Laffont & Tirole (1993) for the treatment of regulated price-setting with participation constraints and incentive compatibility under cost uncertainty.
2. Industrial Organization of Pharmaceutical Markets — focusing on originator vs. generic competition, fixed costs, and market exit thresholds in regulated pricing environments.
3. Public Economics / Optimal Insurance Design — second-best insurance contracting under coinsurance constraints (Zeckhauser 1970, Manning et al. 1987).

## Closest Models and Frameworks

### Model 1: Pauly (1968) Moral Hazard in Health Insurance
- **Type:** Partial equilibrium consumer demand model with insurance subsidy
- **Key feature:** Insurance lowers the effective marginal price of medical care below social marginal cost, causing over-consumption; the key result is that full coverage is never optimal because of moral hazard deadweight loss
- **Difference:** Pauly (1968) treats the price of care as exogenous. This paper endogenizes the price through a VBP procurement mechanism and adds a supply-side participation constraint for the originator firm. The interaction between the insurance-distorted demand and the VBP-set price is the novel feature.
- **Confidence:** HIGH
- **Citation:** Pauly, M.V. (1968). "The Economics of Moral Hazard: Comment." *American Economic Review*, 58(3): 531–537. [VERIFIED]

### Model 2: Zeckhauser (1970) Risk–Incentive Tradeoff in Insurance
- **Type:** Optimal insurance design under moral hazard
- **Key feature:** Derives the optimal coinsurance rate as the solution to the tradeoff between risk sharing (consumer welfare) and incentive efficiency (avoid over-use). The optimal contract has partial coverage, not full.
- **Difference:** Zeckhauser (1970) takes the production side (drug supply) as a competitive market clearing at marginal cost. This paper replaces that with a VBP-regulated price that may lie below the originator's average cost, generating a participation constraint not present in Zeckhauser's framework.
- **Confidence:** HIGH
- **Citation:** Zeckhauser, R. (1970). "Medical Insurance: A Case Study of the Tradeoffs between Risk Spreading and Appropriate Incentives." *Journal of Economic Theory*, 2(1): 10–26. [VERIFIED]

### Model 3: Manning et al. (1987) / RAND Health Insurance Experiment
- **Type:** Empirical demand estimation under randomized coinsurance variation; provides the structural demand elasticity estimates that calibrate moral hazard models
- **Key feature:** Arc-elasticity of medical care demand ≈ −0.2 over the relevant range; modest but meaningful price responsiveness. Full coverage plans generate about 30% more spending than high-cost-sharing plans.
- **Difference:** Manning et al. (1987) is empirical and does not model the supply side or VBP. This paper uses the qualitative finding (demand is inelastic but not perfectly so) to calibrate the sign of the total expenditure response and to justify that moral hazard amplification is real but modest.
- **Confidence:** HIGH
- **Citation:** Manning, W.G., Newhouse, J.P., Duan, N., Keeler, E.B., Leibowitz, A., and Marquis, M.S. (1987). "Health Insurance and the Demand for Medical Care: Evidence from a Randomized Experiment." *American Economic Review*, 77(3): 251–277. [VERIFIED]

### Model 4: Shleifer (1985) Yardstick Competition
- **Type:** Regulatory mechanism design; price set equal to best-practice cost among comparable firms
- **Key feature:** A regulator observing only one firm's cost can use other firms' costs as a yardstick, inducing cost efficiency while satisfying participation constraints. The competitive bidding in China's VBP is structurally similar: the "yardstick price" is the lowest-winning bid among competing generic manufacturers.
- **Difference:** Shleifer (1985) focuses on cost-efficiency incentives for the regulated firm and does not analyze the demand-side moral hazard generated by insurance coverage, nor does it consider the exit of higher-cost firms (originators) that cannot compete at the yardstick price.
- **Confidence:** HIGH
- **Citation:** Shleifer, A. (1985). "A Theory of Yardstick Competition." *RAND Journal of Economics*, 16(3): 319–327. [VERIFIED]

### Model 5: Laffont & Tirole (1993) Procurement Regulation with Adverse Selection
- **Type:** Principal-agent / mechanism design in procurement; regulator designs a menu of contracts for a firm with private cost information
- **Key feature:** Optimal regulated price trades off rent extraction (lower price) against productive efficiency (participation). When the principal is a monopsonist buyer (e.g., government), the optimal price lies above marginal cost to respect the participation constraint.
- **Difference:** Laffont & Tirole (1993) abstract from the demand side entirely — they model the buyer's objective as minimizing procurement cost, not maximizing social welfare including consumers' moral-hazard-distorted demand. This paper combines the Laffont-Tirole procurement side with a Pauly-Zeckhauser demand side, yielding a two-sided distortion model absent from either tradition separately.
- **Confidence:** HIGH
- **Citation:** Laffont, J.-J. and Tirole, J. (1993). *A Theory of Incentives in Procurement and Regulation*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press. [VERIFIED]

### Model 6: Newhouse (1993) RAND HIE Summary
- **Type:** Comprehensive empirical and conceptual synthesis of insurance demand and welfare under the RAND experiment
- **Key feature:** Documents that cost sharing significantly reduces utilization; full insurance generates substantial over-use. Provides the conceptual foundation for treating the coinsurance rate as the key policy lever for managing moral hazard.
- **Difference:** Focuses on the United States fee-for-service market; no VBP, no originator vs. generic distinction, no government monopsony pricing.
- **Confidence:** HIGH
- **Citation:** Newhouse, J.P. (1993). *Free for All? Lessons from the RAND Health Insurance Experiment*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press. [VERIFIED]

## Novelty Dimensions

| # | Claimed Novelty | Risk Level | Rationale |
|---|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| N1 | Joint analysis of demand-side moral hazard and supply-side participation constraint in a single model where the VBP price affects both simultaneously | LOW | Standard moral hazard models (Pauly, Zeckhauser) take supply as competitive; procurement models (Shleifer, Laffont-Tirole) ignore demand-side distortions. The joint framework appears novel. |
| N2 | Characterization of the sign of total insurance expenditure as a function of VBP price, given a fixed coinsurance rate | MEDIUM | The direction of expenditure response is standard in health economics (total spending = p × D(p)); the novelty is deriving the condition in terms of VBP price vs. coinsurance rate jointly, and showing the non-monotone shape when firm exit is present. | N3 | Identification of a critical VBP price p̄ (originator exit threshold) and welfare kink at that price | LOW | The welfare-kink-at-exit-threshold structure does not appear in the health economics literature reviewed. It combines IO/fixed-cost-exit logic with insurance welfare analysis. |
| N4 | Welfare comparison between cost-minimizing VBP price and socially optimal price in a framework with two-sided distortions | MEDIUM | Second-best procurement pricing is studied in Laffont-Tirole; second-best insurance pricing is studied in Zeckhauser. Combining both in a welfare comparison may have partial antecedents in health economics IO. |
| N5 | Application of the joint framework to China's national VBP (集采) as the motivating institutional context | LOW | The China VBP program is primarily studied empirically; no theoretical welfare model of the specific mechanism found in literature search. |

**Overall novelty assessment:**
The core novelty (N1 and N3) is credible: the combination of demand-side moral hazard amplification with supply-side participation-constraint-induced exit — mediated by a VBP-set price — creates a two-sided distortion framework that does not appear to have been formally modeled in either the health economics or procurement regulation literatures. The individual components (moral hazard, participation constraint, regulated pricing) are textbook material, but their interaction in the joint welfare function produces non-trivial results: a welfare function with a kink at the originator exit threshold, possible non-monotonicity in the VBP price, and a systematic divergence between the cost-minimizing procurement price and the social welfare-maximizing price. The paper should be careful to position itself clearly as combining and extending two separate traditions, rather than claiming to introduce these concepts individually.

## Anticipated Referee Objections

### Objection 1
**The referee will say:** "The demand-side result is trivially covered by Pauly (1968). When the price falls, demand rises — there is nothing new here."
**Suggested response:** The paper's contribution is not the demand response in isolation but the interaction with the supply-side participation constraint. The paper shows that the same price reduction that amplifies moral hazard demand also triggers originator exit, producing a welfare kink that cannot be derived from Pauly's model alone. The joint characterization — including the sign of total insurance expenditure as a function of the VBP price and the coinsurance rate, and the welfare comparison at the exit threshold — constitutes the novel contribution.

### Objection 2
**The referee will say:** "The participation constraint is standard Laffont-Tirole. The insurer should just set price above the originator's cost floor to avoid exit."
**Suggested response:** The insurer in China's VBP is a cost-minimizing procurement agent, not a social welfare maximizer. The paper shows formally that the cost-minimizing procurement price systematically lies below the social welfare-maximizing price because the insurer does not internalize: (i) consumer surplus of under-insured patients, (ii) the variety value of originator drugs, and (iii) the dynamic innovation incentive. This divergence — which is the paper's policy message — cannot be derived from Laffont-Tirole alone.

### Objection 3
**The referee will say:** "Reference pricing in Germany and the Netherlands already does exactly what VBP does — there must be a theoretical literature on this."
**Suggested response:** Reference pricing sets a reimbursement ceiling based on cheapest equivalent, but does not involve competitive bidding with volume guarantees. The VBP mechanism is structurally distinct: it combines competitive bidding, volume guarantees, and exclusivity from hospital formularies — features absent from European reference pricing. Any theoretical literature on reference pricing is a partial antecedent; search warranted.

## Positioning Statement

This paper sits at the intersection of two well-established but largely separate theoretical traditions in economics. The first is the moral hazard literature in health insurance, anchored by Arrow (1963), Pauly (1968), and Zeckhauser (1970), which models insurance coverage as a wedge between the private and social marginal cost of care. These models are primarily demand-side: they characterize the optimal coinsurance rate as a function of demand elasticity and risk aversion, taking the price of care as competitively determined or exogenous. The second is the procurement regulation literature, anchored by Shleifer (1985) and Laffont and Tirole (1993), which models a regulator or monopsony buyer setting prices for regulated firms subject to participation constraints and incentive compatibility. These models are primarily supply-side: they characterize the optimal regulated price as a function of cost information, leaving demand largely in the background.

The proposed paper introduces a model that explicitly couples both sides: the VBP-set price affects both the supplier's participation decision (supply side) and the insured patient's demand through the coinsurance channel (demand side). The resulting welfare function has properties that neither tradition predicts individually — in particular, a welfare kink at the originator exit threshold and a systematic gap between the cost-minimizing procurement price and the social welfare-maximizing price. This gap arises from three externalities the cost-minimizing insurer does not internalize: the consumer surplus of marginally-insured or uninsured patients who benefit from VBP prices, the welfare cost of reduced therapeutic variety when originators exit, and the dynamic innovation externality from reduced expected profit.

The closest applied antecedent is the empirical literature on China's National Volume-Based Procurement program. Several recent empirical studies have documented that VBP significantly reduces drug prices (by 50% on average) and reduces drug expenditure in the short run, but has also led to supply chain instability, quality concerns, and anecdotal originator exit from hospital formularies. This paper provides the theoretical framework needed to interpret these empirical patterns and to design VBP price floors that balance fiscal efficiency with market participation and social welfare.
